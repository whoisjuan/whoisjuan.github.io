    Validating With Quantitative Data

    To validate this problem I partnered with my PM. We looked at the raw account and interaction data. Since this was a "pipeline size" problem I suggested we looked into that.

    • equalizer

      P90 of Stages Per Pipeline

      Are all of our customers building long pipelines?

    • equalizer

      P90 of Actions Per Pipeline

      Are our customers building stages with a lot of serial actions?

    • analytics

      Time Spent in Route and Overall Interaction

      Are our customers accomplishing their goals on that view?

    We found that the P90 of stages was 4 stages and the P90 of actions per stage was 15 actions

    We also saw there was a non-proportional increase of time spent in the view as the pipeline size increased.

    How to confirm the problem?

    Confirming Problem

    I created a pipeline based on the P90 size (for stages and actions) and tested its usability on the most popular viewport (1440x900).

    - Problem Scoping -
    What's the Problem?

    Most CodePipeline customers that use the console are suffering navigability and visibility issues related to the layout of the pipeline visualization.

    • unfold_more

      Pipeline Visualization Is Too Long

      Visualization doesn't fit in the viewport. Customers need to scroll multiple folds to reach the bottom.

    • remove_red_eye

      Hard to Determine Pipeline Status

      Current status and overall structure of a pipeline is hard to determine at first glance (cognitive load).

    - Problem Scoping -
    Determining a Solution Direction

    • "This console just got migrated to Polaris (the new design system). You need to fix the problem without compromising the design system."

      Peer Product Designer AWS Polaris Team @Berlin

      • First Glance

        Provide first glance visibility of the pipeline status irrespective of its size.

      • Help with Scrolling

        Create a mechanism to help users scroll with ease.

      • Feedback Loop

        Give users clear feedback on where they are when seeing a pipeline.

    - Conceptualizing Solution -
    Using an analogous problem to to conceptualize a solution.

    One area of complaint from developers when it comes to density is visualizing code in websites, IDEs, etc.

    Many developers prefer high density when reading and typing code. How do code editors and similar products give high-level visibility (length of file, errors, etc)?

    - Designing a Solution -
    Low-Fi Sketches

    Getting Ready for User Testing

    In classic Amazon fashion, I prepared a document with all my research and findings. Meeting with PM, Software Manager of service team, Software Manager of DX (Frontend) team, and relevant developers. We decided best proposals to test.

    I conducted testing with five CodePipeline customers using mid-fi wireframes/prototype built with ImageMaps/JS (to simulate the scrolling interactions).

    • Mid-Fi: Top Sticky Summary

      - Users had a hard time grasping the top summary concept.
      - After some struggle, they usually will express some excitement about the solution.
      - They thought it wasn't necessary to show all detail

      3 of 6 participants easily completed the task.

      3 of 6 participants had some struggle to complete the task.

    • Mid-Fi: Side Sticky Scroller

      - Users got the concept almost immediately. Specially after the first scroll attempt.
      - Discoverability of the interactions wasn't optimal, but eventually all customers discovered the interactions.
      - All participants thought this solution showed the right amount of information.

      5 of 6 participants easily completed the task.

      1 of 6 participants had some struggle to complete the task, but he said that this experience solved his problem.

    • Picking the Winner and Creating Final Mockups with AWS BobRoss

      After the testing it was clear that the appropriate solution was the Side Sticky Stage Summary with Scrolling Interactions.

      At this point, the final step is delivering the final mockups and requirements to the DX (Frontend Team). For this task I used BobRoss.

      BobRoss is an isolated React Implementation of the Polaris Design System.
      BobRoss allows you to create code layouts, produce realistic mockups and even share markup in the hand-off process.

    After all the details and polish were finalized we shipped the solution and start gathering data and collecting feeback.

    GitLab Visual Review Tool

    1 - Context

    GitLab MVC Methodology and the Visual Review Tool

    GitLab uses the Minimum Viable Change methodology which consists in shippping the smallest possible iteration to solve a particular problem.

    When I joined GitLab I was tasked with an MVC revamp of a little power tool known as the Visual Review Tool (a widget developers can embed in their apps so other can leave feedback without opening the MR in GitLab)

    I worked with a PM and a Frontend Developer to deliver visual and usability improvements for this tool.

    Existing Issues in The Review Tool.

    The first part of the process was conducting a usability analysis and identifying opportunities for improvement as well as solving gruesome UX issues. Here are my initial findings.

    • Widget Position in Viewport

      Bottom Right is a potentially conflicting location for the widget since many consumer apps now feature a support chat client on that area..

    • Icon and Affordance

      Icon is vague. It can be confusing especially because many support chat clients look also use similar iconography.

    • Requesting Information with No Context

      Users may not have enough context on why or how to enter a merge request ID. They might be seeing this widget for the first time.

    Existing Issues in The Review Tool.

    The first part of the process was conducting a usability analysis and identifying opportunities for improvement as well as solving gruesome UX issues. Here are my initial findings.

    • Poor Click Boundaries

      Links that are too close can lead to unintended clicks . This needs better spacing or a different display logic.

    • Un-Optimal Text Box

      The text field is the most important part of the tool. The real estate of the field in the overall box layout can be improved to optimize readability and length.

    • Un-Optimal Real Estate Usage

      The collapse button takes a whole column creating a empty pocket of unused real estate creating an unoptimal distrubution of the elements.

    Existing Issues in The Review Tool.

    The first part of the process was conducting a usability analysis and identifying opportunities for improvement as well as solving gruesome UX issues. Here are my initial findings.

    • Short-lived Snack Notifications

      The confirmation dialogue is temporary (10s). User loses the context of the presented information which could lead to repeated submissions.

    • Poor Error States

      The error message position is not optimal and the messaging in general is vague and not helpful.

    • Improvements

      After socializing my findings and confirming the problems I created a series of improvements and polishes for the GitLab Visual Review Tool.

      • Improved Widget Position and Affordances
      • Improved Initial Context and Spacing
      • Improved Information Presentation and Behavior
      • Improved Error States and Snack Messages

    Implementation and Iteration.

    As mentioned initially all these changes happen through small merge requests so not all the suggestions are in production at the curent time.

    However, we have shipped some of the most significant ones and keep iterating on this tool as we get feedback from customers and usage data.

    We believe this feature can helps us drive adoption of testing features like Review Apps so we will keep iterating on upcoming milestones.
